About

Stars, signals, and students

I am an astronomer and professor in the Department of Astronomy and Astrophysics at the University of Chicago. My work spans stellar observations, asteroseismology, magnetic activity, exoplanets, and astronomical instrumentation, with a particular interest in using precise time-series measurements to understand how stars behave.

Public profiles list earlier roles and collaborations connected with space-based stellar photometry and ultraviolet/optical instrumentation, including WIRE, Kepler, TESS, and mission concept work for massive-star seismology. Research

Research interests

Starter text below; each card can become a fuller project page later.

Asteroseismology & stellar variability

Stellar oscillations reveal the internal structure of stars. I work with high-precision photometry and time-series methods to identify and interpret oscillation signals across a range of stellar types.

TESS, WIRE & space photometry

Space missions can deliver the long, precise, nearly continuous light curves needed for asteroseismology and activity studies. Current interests include extracting robust stellar signals from TESS data, including bright and saturated targets.

Massive stars and UV diagnostics

Massive stars are short-lived, influential, and observationally demanding. Ultraviolet and optical measurements can help constrain oscillations, winds, atmospheres, and the physics of hot stars.

Activity, rotation & exoplanet hosts

Stellar activity and rotation shape what we infer about stars and their planets. Solar analogs, gyrochronology, and activity diagnostics connect stellar astrophysics with exoplanet characterization.
